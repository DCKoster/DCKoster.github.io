Variations

4 Person Variation

Our 4 person variation of the ”Who has the sum?” goes as follows (the differences are in bold):

Achmed, Bert, Carl, and Dana all have a positive integer on their forehead. They can only see the foreheads of others. One of the numbers is the sum of the other three. None of the numbers are larger than 40. All the previous is common knowledge, all agents are perfect logicians, they always speak the truth, which is also common knowledge. They now successively make the truthful announcements:

  1. Achmed: “I don’t know my number.”
  2. Bert: “I don’t know my number.”
  3. Carl: “I don’t know my number.”
  4. Dana: “I don’t know my number.”
  5. Achmed: “I know my number. It is NUM4.”

What are the other numbers?

The public announcements are now formally as follows:

  1. \( \neg K_a 1_a \land \neg K_a 2_a \land \ldots \land \neg K_a x_a \) (for all \( x \in \mathbb{Z}^+ \))
  2. \( \neg K_b 1_b \land \neg K_b 2_b \land \ldots \land \neg K_b x_b \) (for all \( x \in \mathbb{Z}^+ \))
  3. \( \neg K_c 1_c \land \neg K_c 2_c \land \ldots \land \neg K_c x_c \) (for all \( x \in \mathbb{Z}^+ \))
  4. \( \neg K_d 1_d \land \neg K_d 2_d \land \ldots \land \neg K_d x_d \) (for all \( x \in \mathbb{Z}^+ \))
  5. \( K_a NUM4_a \)

The differences with the 3 person riddle are, summarised:

  1. There are 4 agents rather than 3.
  2. There are 5 public announcements rather than 4.
  3. Achmed’s number is not 50, but NUM4.
  4. None of the numbers are larger than 40.

For the model itself, each world w now represents 4 numbers \( n_a, n_b, n_c,\) and \( n_d )\ and is referenced as \( (n_a, n_b, n_c, n_d) \). Each world has 4 relations \( \sim_a, \sim_b, \sim_c \), and \( \sim_d \) with other worlds, because the agents know they have either the sum or the difference of the other three numbers (the largest number they see minus the sum of the two smallest numbers they see). In contrast to the 3 person riddle, and in contrast to definition 3, a root world for the 4 person variation is defined as such:

For the riddle “Who has the Sum” with 4 agents, a world is a root world iff the difference between the second largest number and the sum of the two smallest numbers is smaller than or equal to 0. (6)

Somewhat similar to the 3 person riddle, four of the root nodes are /( (3, 1, 1, 1) \), /( (1, 3, 1, 1) \), /( (1, 1, 3, 1) \), and /( (1, 1, 1, 3) \), where the world /( w = (na, nb, nc, nd) \) follows the pattern /( (3x, x, x, x) \) for any order of /( na, nb, nc, \) and \( nd \). However, there are more than these. In fact, there is an unlimited number of root nodes. For example, all worlds that follow the pattern /( (2x, x, y, z) \), such as /( (4, 2, 1, 1) \), are root nodes. And there are many root nodes that do not follow such a specific pattern, which makes it so that there is an unlimited number of models that are not bisimilar. This is why we added to the riddle that none of the numbers are larger than 40.

In regards to our research question of whether a Kripke Model will still be efficient when implemented on variants of “Who has the Sum?” if fundamental changes are made to the riddle, the fundamental change we make here is that instead of 3 root nodes, there is much larger number of them.

While for the 3 person variation the worlds w where k > 4 in r →k w are irrelevant, for the 4 person variation the worlds w where k > 5 in r →k w are irrelevant. This is because there is one more announcement.

To complete the strategy for this variation, the task is to create the model that contains all root nodes, but with only the worlds w where k ≤ 5 in r →k w, to reduce the model with the public announcements, to find in the final reduced model the world where Achmed knows his number is a multiple of NUM4 ( \( K_a x_a \) for any x and y where \( x * y = NUM4 \) ), and to finally note the numbers of the others in that world \( (n_b, n_c, \) and \( n_d) \).
